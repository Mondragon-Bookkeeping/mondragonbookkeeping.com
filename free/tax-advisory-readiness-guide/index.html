Most people come to a tax advisor
too late

Not too late in life — too late in the year. They show up in February with a shoebox of receipts and hope for the best. By then, most of the opportunities to actually reduce their tax bill have already passed.

"Tax preparation is looking backward. Tax advisory is looking forward. The best results happen when you do both — year-round."

5 questions every business owner should be able to answer about their tax situation  ·  The real difference between a tax preparer and a tax advisor  ·  A year-round calendar of what proactive tax planning actually looks like  ·  Common missed opportunities by situation type  ·  How to prepare for your first advisory conversation

5 questions every business owner
should be able to answer

These aren't trick questions. But if you can't answer them confidently, it's a signal that there's real opportunity being left on the table.

Question 01
"Do you know your effective tax rate — and what's driving it?"
Why it matters

Most people know roughly what they paid last year. Far fewer know why they paid that amount — and what could change it. Your effective rate is the starting point for every planning conversation. If you don't know it, you can't measure whether your strategy is working.

→ Advisory goal: Understand and reduce this number year over year
Question 02
"Are you structured correctly for the income you're earning?"
Why it matters

Sole prop, LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp — each has different tax implications, especially around self-employment tax. A business earning $150k as a sole prop often pays $20k+ more in taxes than the same business structured as an S-Corp. Entity structure is one of the highest-leverage decisions you'll make.

→ Advisory goal: Confirm your structure matches your income level and goals
Question 03
"Are you making estimated quarterly tax payments — and are they accurate?"
Why it matters

Underpaying estimated taxes triggers IRS penalties before you even file. Overpaying means you've been giving the government an interest-free loan all year. Getting this dialed in keeps cash flow predictable and eliminates April surprises.

→ Advisory goal: Accurate quarterly payments — no surprises, no penalties

Question 04
"Do you know which deductions you qualify for — and can you document them?"
Why it matters

The tax code is full of legitimate deductions that most people miss — not because they don't qualify, but because they don't know to look for them or haven't kept the documentation to support them. Home office, vehicle use, retirement contributions, Section 179 depreciation, cost segregation on real estate — these add up fast. An audit without documentation is a deduction lost.

→ Advisory goal: Identify every deduction you qualify for and build the paper trail
Question 05
"What is your tax plan for next year — right now?"
Why it matters

This is the question that separates reactive clients from proactive ones. If your answer is "I haven't thought about it yet," that's the most expensive position to be in. The strategies that make the biggest difference — maxing retirement accounts, timing income and deductions, adjusting withholding, making charitable moves — only work if you plan for them before December 31st.

→ Advisory goal: A written plan with action steps before year-end, every year
The honest truth

If you could answer all five of these confidently, you likely wouldn't need this guide. Most business owners can answer one or two. The gap between what you're doing and what's possible is where a good advisor earns their fee — many times over.

Reactive vs. Proactive:
the real difference

Most people have a tax preparer. Far fewer have a tax advisor. Here's what that distinction actually looks like in practice.

A Tax Preparer… A Tax Advisor…
Files your return accurately based on what happened Plans ahead to change what happens before year-end
Contacts you once a year, at tax time Is in your corner year-round — not just April
Tells you what you owe Works to reduce what you'll owe next year
Applies standard deductions Actively hunts for every deduction you qualify for
Reviews your situation once a year Monitors changes in your life and adjusts your strategy
Tells you your entity structure is "fine" Analyzes whether a restructure would save you money
Sends you a bill after filing Charges a flat rate — no surprises, no hourly billing
"Your accountant should be saving you more than they cost you. If they're not — that's worth examining."

The advisory difference in numbers: A business owner earning $200k/year who switches from reactive to proactive tax management typically saves $8,000–$25,000+ annually through entity optimization, retirement strategy, deduction maximization, and timing. The higher the income and the more complexity in your situation, the larger that number gets.

What proactive tax planning
actually looks like

A year-round calendar of what you should be thinking about — and what a good advisor should be doing for you.

January – March
▸Gather and review prior-year documents
▸File or extend personal & business returns
▸Review prior-year advisory notes — are you on track?
▸Issue W-2s and 1099s (January)
April – June
▸Q1 estimated tax payment due (April 15)
▸Review Q1 financials — P&L, cash flow
▸Mid-year entity structure review
▸Q2 estimated payment due (June 15)
July – September
▸Mid-year tax planning check-in
▸Review withholding — adjust if needed
▸Retirement contribution strategy review
▸Q3 estimated payment due (Sept 15)
October – December
▸Year-end tax planning — most critical window
▸Income/deduction timing decisions
▸Max retirement accounts before Dec 31
▸Charitable giving strategy (DAF, bunching)
▸Q4 estimated payment due (Jan 15)
The December 31st rule

Most tax-saving strategies have a hard deadline: December 31st of the tax year. After that date, your options to reduce that year's liability are nearly zero. This is why year-round advisory matters. Everything that changes your tax bill happens before you file — not during.

What to think about
for your situation

Tax strategy isn't one-size-fits-all. Here are the most commonly missed opportunities by situation type — and the questions worth bringing to an advisor.

Business Owner
▸Is your entity structure optimized for self-employment tax?
▸Are you maximizing retirement contributions (Solo 401k, SEP IRA)?
▸Are you tracking all deductible business expenses?
▸Do you have a written year-end tax plan?
▸Are your books clean enough to support a deduction if audited?
Rental Property Owner
▸Are you capturing all allowable rental deductions?
▸Have you considered a cost segregation study?
▸Do you qualify as a real estate professional (REP status)?
▸Are your passive activity losses being tracked properly?
▸Is your depreciation schedule accurate and up to date?
Investor
▸Are you timing gains and losses strategically (tax-loss harvesting)?
▸Are you holding assets long enough for long-term capital gains rates?
▸How are crypto transactions being tracked and reported?
▸Is your charitable giving strategy optimized (DAF, appreciated assets)?
Before your first advisory conversation — bring these
Last 2 years of filed tax returns
Current business structure / formation docs
Most recent P&L and balance sheet
Estimated income for this year
List of current retirement accounts
Any major financial events planned (sale, raise, hire)
Your biggest tax frustration or unanswered question
Your financial goals for the next 12 months
